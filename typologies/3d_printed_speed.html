More about Binder Jetting

How Binder Jetting Works:

  • Preparation: The process begins with a thin layer of powdered material (such as metals, ceramics, or polymers) spread evenly across the build platform.
  • Inkjet Printing: A print head moves over the powdered bed and selectively deposits a liquid binding agent onto specific areas of the powder layer.
    • The binding agent acts like a glue, binding the powder particles together in the desired pattern corresponding to the cross-section of the 3D object being printed.
  • Layer-by-Layer Build: After each layer is selectively bonded, a new layer of powder is spread across the build platform, and the process repeats.
  • Post-Processing: Once printing is complete, the part undergoes post-processing to remove excess powder and finalize its properties.

Key Features and Advantages:

  • Material Versatility: Binder jetting supports a wide range of materials, including metals, ceramics, and polymers, offering flexibility for various applications.
  • High-Speed Printing: Compared to some other additive manufacturing methods, binder jetting can achieve relatively high printing speeds due to the simultaneous processing of entire layers.
  • Economical: It can be cost-effective for producing complex geometries or large-scale parts because it does not require support structures like other methods.
  • Scalability: Binder jetting is suitable for both prototyping and production applications, allowing for scalability from small batches to large-volume production.

Applications of Binder Jetting:

  • Prototyping: Rapid iteration and testing of designs in various materials without the need for expensive tooling.
  • Customization: Production of customized parts and components tailored to specific applications or individual requirements.
  • Tooling: Manufacturing of tooling and fixtures used in industrial processes.
  • Medical and Dental: Production of patient-specific implants and prosthetics.
  • Art and Design: Creation of intricate sculptures and artistic pieces.

Considerations:

  • Resolution: Binder jetting may have lower resolution compared to other methods like selective laser sintering (SLS), affecting surface finish and dimensional accuracy.
  • Material Properties: The mechanical properties of parts produced by binder jetting can vary based on material choice and post-processing techniques.
  • Powder Handling: Handling of powders, including storage, recycling, and safety considerations, is critical in binder jetting operations.

In summary, binder jetting is a versatile additive manufacturing process suitable for a wide range of materials and applications. It combines speed, cost-effectiveness, and scalability, making it an attractive option for both prototyping and production in various industries.

How Selective Laser Sintering (SLS) Works

Preparation: A thin layer of powdered material (such as plastics, ceramics, or metals) is evenly spread over the build platform.

Laser Sintering: A high-power laser scans the surface of the powdered material, selectively sintering (heating and fusing) the particles together based on a 3D digital model of the object being created.

Layer-by-Layer Build: After sintering a layer, the build platform moves down by one layer thickness, and a new layer of powder is spread on top. The laser then sinters the new layer to the previous one. This process repeats until the entire object is formed.

Cooling and Post-Processing: Once printing is complete, the build chamber cools down, and the excess, unsintered powder serves as support for the printed part. After cooling, the part is removed from the powder bed, and excess powder is brushed off. Further post-processing may include heat treatment or surface finishing.

How Selective Laser Melting (SLM) Works

Preparation: Metal powder (such as titanium, aluminum, stainless steel) is spread in a thin layer on the build platform.

Laser Melting: A high-power laser beam selectively melts the metal powder particles together at the points defined by the 3D model. The laser quickly heats the powder to the melting point, creating a fully dense metal part.

Layer-by-Layer Build: After each layer is melted, the build platform moves down, and a new layer of powder is spread on top. The laser sinters this layer to the previous one, gradually building up the part.

Cooling and Post-Processing: After printing, the metal part remains within the powder bed to cool. Excess powder is removed through brushing and possibly sieving to recover unused powder for reuse. The printed metal part may undergo further heat treatment or machining.
